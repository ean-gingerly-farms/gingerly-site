Why Water Matters

Hydroponics uses up to 90% less water than traditional farming. Here's what that looks like in practice and why it matters for the Lowcountry.

Gingerly Farms/4 min read/March 2026

The Lowcountry is defined by water. Tidal creeks, salt marshes, the harbor, the rivers that feed it. Water is part of the identity here, and part of the responsibility. When we started thinking about how to grow food in Charleston, water was one of the first things we looked at.

Traditional field farming for leafy greens uses a lot of it. Irrigation, runoff, evaporation. In a typical outdoor operation, most of the water you put in doesn't actually reach the plant. It goes into the ground, runs off the surface, or evaporates before it does any good.

The Hydroponic Difference

In our system, water is recirculated. The nutrient solution flows past the roots, gets collected, checked, adjusted if needed, and sent back through. Very little is lost. The main water consumption comes from what the plants actually take up and transpire, which is the whole point.

For context, growing a head of lettuce in a field typically takes 15 to 25 gallons of water from start to harvest. In our NFT system, that same plant takes roughly 2 to 4 gallons. The difference compounds fast when you scale up production.

No Runoff, No Residue

The other side of the water equation is what leaves the farm. In conventional agriculture, rain and irrigation carry fertilizer and pesticide residues off the field and into local waterways. In coastal areas like ours, that runoff feeds algal blooms and degrades the marsh ecosystems that define the Lowcountry landscape.

Our closed-loop system produces zero agricultural runoff. Nutrients stay in the system. Nothing flows out into Shem Creek or the Ashley River. For a farm operating in a coastal watershed, that's a design decision we feel good about.

Freshwater in a Coastal Region

Charleston doesn't lack for rainfall, but freshwater supply is still a consideration as the region grows. Saltwater intrusion into groundwater aquifers is a real and increasing challenge along the coast. Every gallon of freshwater used in agriculture is a gallon not available for drinking water, ecosystems, or other needs.

Using 90% less water isn't just a nice number for a slide deck. It's a meaningful operational choice when you're growing in a place where freshwater resources are shared and finite. We didn't build a hydroponic farm to solve the water crisis, but we did want to build something that doesn't make it worse.

Practical, Not Perfect

Hydroponics isn't a silver bullet. Our systems use electricity to run pumps and climate controls. The nutrient solutions are manufactured. There are trade-offs in any method of growing food, and we try to be clear-eyed about ours.

But on the water question specifically, the case is pretty straightforward. Less in, none out, same yield. In a place built on water, that feels like the right approach.
